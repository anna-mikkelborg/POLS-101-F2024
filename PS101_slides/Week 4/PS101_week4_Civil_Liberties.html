Evolution of freedom of speech

  • 1798 Alien and Sedition Acts
  • 1917 Espionage Act
  • 1918 Sedition Act
  • 1919 Schenck v. United States
  • 1950s Red Scare and McCarthy hearings

  • Sedition Act of 1798 made it illegal to make “false or malicious” statements about the federal government

  • Used to suppress speech by the anti-Federalists

  • Expired in 1800- New York Times co v Sullivan, 1964: “Although the Sedition Act was never tested in Court, the attack upon its validity has carried the day in the court of history.”

  • Espionage Act of 1917 made it illegal to disseminate information that could hurt the war effort during WWI. It was enforced in a very aggressive way, and 74 newspapers critical of the war were denied access to the postal service as a result (https://firstamendment.mtsu.edu/article/espionage-act-of-1917/)

  • Sedition Act of 1918 limited citizens’ free speech rights during wartime. It was an amendment to the Espionage Act of 1917. It prohibited individuals from inciting disloyalty to the military, use language that was “disloyal to the government, the Constitution, the military or the flag,” advocate strikes on wartime production, or support countries at war with the U.S. (https://firstamendment.mtsu.edu/article/sedition-act-of-1918/).

  • Charles Schenck and co-defendent Elizabeth Baer, both Socialist Party members, were convicted for violating the Espionage Act for “sending literature to recently conscripted soldiers suggesting that the draft was a form of involuntary servitude that violated the Thirteenth Amendment” The Supreme Court upheld the conviction and allow wartime constraints on the First Amendment to continue

  • Red Scare: post-WWII, as Cold War began, was a moral panic that communists were infiltrating and subverting US government. (There actually was a lot of Soviet espionage in the US during this time.) MCarthy was the chair of the Committee on Government Operations of the Senate in the 1950s and led a series of high-profile hearings on the communist affiliations of various people, including many who were falsely accused or only very loosely connected to communism. FBI head J. Edgar Hoover was also involved in this effort.

A crash course in student speech case law

Tinker v. Des Moines (1969)

  • Students at a public high school wore black armbands to protest Vietnam War
  • School instituted a ban on armbands and suspended students when they wouldn’t remove them
  • Constitutional question: did the prohibition violate students’ freedom of speech protections?
  • Supreme Court ruling: Yes. Schools must prove that speech would “materially and substantially interfere” with normal school activities.

Important quote

“It can hardly be argued that either students or teachers shed their constitutional rights to freedom of speech or expression at the schoolhouse gate.”

Healy v. James (1972)

  • Students at Central Connecticut State College wanted to form a chapter of Students for a Democratic Society
  • CCSC refused to recognize the campus chapter of SDS because chapters elsewhere had staged Civil Rights and anti-Vietnam protests
  • Constitutional question: Does the First Amendment cover activity at public universities as it does elsewhere?
  • Supreme Court ruling: Yes.

Important quotes

“State colleges and universities are not enclaves immune from the sweep of the First Amendment… the precedents of this Court leave no room for the view that… First Amendment protections should apply with less force on college campuses than in the community at large.”

“The college classroom with its surrounding environs is peculiarly the marketplace of ideas.”

Papish v. Board of Curators of the University of Missouri (1973)

  • A student newspaper published the headline “M***F***er Acquitted” and reprinted a political cartoon depicting sexual assault
  • The student responsible for publishing the was expelled
  • Constitutional question: Were the student’s First Amendment rights violated?
  • Supreme Court ruling: Yes. The materials were not “obscene” in a legal sense and there was not a legitimate “time, place, and manner” restriction.

Important quote

“Mere dissemination of ideas—no matter how offensive to good taste—on a state university campus may not be shut off in the name alone of ‘conventions of decency.’”

UWM Post v. Bd. of Regents of University of Wisconsin (1991)

  • The University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee enacted rules banning speech that is “demeaning” on the basis of “race, sex, religion, color, creed, disability, sexual orientation, national origin, ancestry or age.”
  • The student newspaper filed a lawsuit against the school
  • Constitutional question: Did UWM’s hate speech code violate students’ First Amendment rights?
  • Supreme Court decision: Yes. The university’s code was too broad and vague.

Important quote

“Above all else, the First Amendment means that the government has no power to restrict expression because of its message, its ideas, its subject matter, or its content.”

Iota Xi Chapter of Sigma Chi Fraternity v. George Mason University (1993)

  • A student wore black face at a fraternity party
  • At students’ request, the dean disciplined the student
  • Constitutional question: Was disciplining the student a violation of their First Amendment rights?
  • Supreme Court decision: Yes.

Important quote

“If there is a bedrock principle underlying the First Amendment, it is that the Government may not prohibit the expression of an idea simply because society finds the idea itself offensive or disagreeable.”

  • Offensive speech is protected

Free speech and academic freedom

Academic freedom for faculty

  • Not a constitutional right, but a longstanding set of principles in American higher education
  • Faculty have the right to study and teach on controversial topics
  • Tenure system protects faculty members’ academic freedom

CSU instructors are “entitled to learn and to teach in the classroom what scholarship suggests is the truth in their particular field of expertise.”

Academic freedom for students

  • Free expression in the classroom
    • Classroom speech \(\neq\) public speech
  • Protection against improper academic evaluation
  • Protection against improper disclosure

“CSU affirms that academic freedom means that no student should be penalized because of political opinions that differ from a professor’s. Every student should feel comfortable in the right to listen critically and challenge a professor’s opinions.”

Closing thoughts

  • Speech on college campuses has special significance
  • Because of that, it’s especially politicized
  • Campus speech controversies highlight the tensions that arise when individuals exercise their rights within a community
